LinkedIn connection acceptance rates have dropped steadily for three years running, mostly because everyone runs the same three-message sequence: connect, pitch immediately, follow up once, give up. Prospects have learned to recognize it instantly.

## Why the standard sequence stopped working

When every cold connection request is followed by an immediate pitch, the prospect's brain pattern-matches "salesperson" before reading a word of the message. The fix isn't a cleverer pitch — it's removing the pitch from the first few touches entirely.

## The sequence we run instead

- **Touch 1 — connection request, no message.** A blank request converts better than a pitchy one; let the profile do the talking.
- **Touch 2 — a genuine, specific comment** on something they posted or a company update, sent 2-3 days after acceptance.
- **Touch 3 — a low-commitment question**, not a pitch: something tied to a real problem their role would face.
- **Touch 4 — the actual offer**, framed around the answer (or silence) from touch 3.

This stretches the sequence to 10-14 days instead of 3, which feels slower but converts at meaningfully higher rates because the prospect has context before the ask arrives.

## Profile optimization is half the work

None of this works if the sender's profile looks like a lead-gen account. We rebuild every client rep's profile before launching a single sequence: a headline that states a real value proposition instead of a job title, a banner that reinforces it, and a featured section with actual proof — case studies, testimonials, or a short video.

> A prospect checks your profile before deciding whether to reply. If it looks like a sales account, the message doesn't matter.

## Measuring what's working

Track connection acceptance rate and reply rate separately — a sequence can have a great acceptance rate and a terrible reply rate if the messaging after the connect is off. We re-test message variants on a rolling two-week cycle, never letting a sequence run unchanged for more than that.
